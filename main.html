Introduction to Synchronization (part I)

[http://www.scholarpedia.org/article/Synchronization,Synchronization] is found throughout science and engineering (see, e.g. [http://books.google.com/books?id=FuIv845q3QUC,link] and [http://books.google.com/books?id=a4bfvTMvYpcC, link]). Synchronization of cicada (biology) and clocks (physics) and clapping (psycology) are well known. In mobile phones, the synchronization of a high-frequency (GHz) oscillator to a low-frequency (MHz) reference is essential [http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase-locked_loop, link]. While the detail of synchronization is technical, the basic ideas can be quickly grasped wih a modest understanding of dynamic systems. What do such diverse examples of synchronization (from physics, engineering, biology, psycology, ...) have in common? What are the *universal* properties of synchronization (that do not depend on the specifics of a particular system)---and how can they be understood at an intuitive level? To address these questions (at least, to make a start) we consider the most simple case---phase synchronization of an oscillator by an enternal force at the same frequency.
What as "oscillator" is depends on your background. In electronics, an oscillator typically comprises an inductor and capacitor, where (electrical) energy in the capacitor is periodically exchanged with (magnetic) energy in the inductor. Energy sloshes back an forth at the resonant frequency, and a power supply compensates for the inevitable losses. In other fields the details differ --- but the important aspects are the same. A metronome periodically exchanges kinetic energy and potential energy as the pendulum swings --- and its escapement replaces any losses. The main thing is that an "oscillator" has two states (say capacitor voltage and inductor current, or pendulum height and speed), and from any initial condition we end up with a stable, periodic oscillation. Then, plotting one against the other in a [http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_portrait,phase portrait], we see a limit cycle. That is, a repetitive, periodic trajectory of states that attract a range of initial conditions.

Van der Pol Oscillator

The [http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_der_Pol_oscillator,Van der Pol oscillator] is just complex enough to be a [http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toy_problem,toy problem] for synchronization: $$ \frac{d^2x}{dt^2}-\mu \left(1-x^2 \right) \frac{dx}{dt} + x = 0 $$ If we set one state to be $x$ and the other $y=dx/dt$ we find the state behavior shown on the lower left (states as a function of time appear on the lower right). The phase-space is illustrated by the colored dots, which show the trajectory of random initial states for a finite time. The black dot shows a single initial condition that has become trapped on the limit-cycle. The slider controls $\mu$, the nonlinearity---$\mu=0$ corresponds to simple harmonic motion (without a limit cycle), and large $\mu$ tends towards a [http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relaxation_oscillator, relaxation oscillator]. To study synchronization its easiest to consider quasi-linear operation with $\mu<1$ --- where the limit cycle tends towards the grey circle.

External Force

An "enternal force" on an oscillator is something that disturbs its trajectory in the phase space. In practical terms, for instance, electronic oscillators are often disturbed by coupling via finite ground impedances.
Note, though, that this is different from Huygens classic example of two coupled clocks --- in this case the oscillators (clocks) mutually affect each other. The influence of an external force is thus only in one direction -- the oscillator does not affect the force.

Synchronization

We now turn to phase synchronization armed with a critical idea: only disturbaces tangential to the limit cycle are important. To explore the consequences lets consider the effect of a vertical, sinusoidal disturbance on the circular limit-cycle on the right (shown as a vertical arrow). The period of the disturbance matches the oscillator (the time taken to traverse the limit-cycle)---but the *phase* of the disturbance is arbitrary. The red marker shows the point where the disturbance has a positive peak (i.e., shows the phase of the disturbance relative to the limit-cycle). The green trace shows the tangential disturbance, which either encourages the oscillator phase to move in a clockwise, or anti-clockwise, direction. To make things easy, lets decouple the distubance from the oscillator and study the effect that it *would* have as we vary its phase. If we first [reset] the oscillator so that the peak disturbance is at point A, it is visually obvious (from the arrow as it traverses the limit cycle) that the oscillator phase would be pulled in a clockwise direction.
oscillate! stop osc! Lets assume then that the [oscillator increses by phase 45 degrees] -- as a result of the disturbance. Now the green trace---which was previously entirely negative---is no partly positive. Averaged over the course of a full cycle there is less anti-clockwise force from the disturbance than before. What if we increase the oscillator phase by a [further 90 degrees]? Now we see that the green trace is mostly positive! Indicating a net clock-wise force on the oscillator phase over a full cycle. Clearly, the oscillator phase is being encouraged by the disturbance to a point where the [average force over a full period is zero]. It is instructive to increment the oscillator phase clockwise or anti-clockwise and observe the green trace. In every case the oscillator phase is encouraged back to the same point. Thus, whatever the phase of the oscillator is initially relative to the disturbance, the disturbance will encourage the oscillator to a fixed phase---in this case, so that the oscillator is at the top of the limit-cycle when the disturbance has its peak positive value. In other words, the oscillator phase-locks to the disturbance.
