Examples of synchronization are found throughout science and engineering X. Synchronization of cicada (biology X) and clocks (physics X) and clapping (psycology X) are well known. In mobile phones, the synchronization of a high-frequency (GHz) oscillator to a low-frequency (MHz) reference is essential. While the detail of synchronization may be *very* technical, it is quite possible to quicky appreciate its broad ideas with an intuitive grasp of dynamic systems (limit cycles in particular). Here we follow the first part of [X], "Synchronization without formulae". The question (as ever) is what do such differing examples of synchronization (from physics, engineering, biology, psycology, ...) have in common? That is, what are the *universal* properties of synchronization that do not depend on a particular system? To address these questions (at least, to make a start) we consider the most simple case --- an oscillator synchonized by an enternal force.
What as "oscillator" is to you will depend on your background. In electronics, an oscillator typically comprises an inductor and capacitor, where (electrical) energy in the capacitor is periodically exchanged with (magnetic) energy in the inductor. Energy sloshes back an forth at the resonant frequency, and a power supply compensates for the inevitable losses. In other fields the details will differ --- but the important aspects are the same. For example, a metronome periodically exchanges kinetic energy and potential energy as the pendulum swings --- and its escapement replaces any losses. For us, the main thing is that an "oscillator" has two states (say capacitor voltage and inductor current, or pendulum height and speed), and from any initial condition we end up with a stable, periodic oscillation. Furthermore, because each state is periodic, if we plot one against the other in a phase plane we see a limit cycle.
